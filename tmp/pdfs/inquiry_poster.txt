Cutoff Physics

Research Question:
How can a real-time aircraft routing algorithm that exploits
Mach-cutoff physics over land minimize travel time while
keeping population exposure to noise below a defined
parameter for supersonic overland flights?

Problem Statement:
Supersonic aircraft generate shock waves that
usually reach the ground as loud sonic booms,
which has led to strict regulations banning civilian
supersonic flight over land. However, under
certain atmospheric conditions, sound‐speed
(refractive index) gradients bend these shock
waves upward so they never reach the ground.
This phenomenon, known as Mach cutoff, depends
on real-world atmospheric behavior: temperature
profiles, wind direction, and pressure gradients.
With modern high-resolution atmospheric datasets
like ERA5 and HRRR, it may be possible to
predict Mach cutoff zones in real time. If so,
supersonic aircraft could plan routes that fly above
populated areas only when the atmosphere
naturally prevents sonic booms from reaching the
ground.

Hypothesis:
I believe that Mach cutoff–aware routing will significantly
reduce predicted sonic-boom exposure compared to traditional
supersonic paths.
However, I also expect that cutoff zones will not always align
with optimal routes, meaning flight efficiency may be reduced
in some atmospheric conditions, potentially being even worse
than subsonic routes.
A likely outcome is that cutoff routing provides strong benefits
in certain high-gradient atmospheric profiles, especially when
no population is overflown, but offers limited improvement in
others.

Methodological Justification and Analysis Type:

Value of Research:

This study employs a quantitative, simulation-based methodology because the research question centers
on evaluating measurable trade-offs between supersonic flight performance and ground-level sonic boom
exposure. Both sonic boom propagation and aircraft motion are governed by well-defined physical and
mathematical relationships, making numerical modeling the most appropriate approach for isolating
cause-and-effect relationships.

This research matters because it addresses a critical
barrier to the reintroduction of civilian supersonic
flight: community impact from sonic booms. While
much prior work emphasizes aircraft design
modifications, this study demonstrates that
operational strategies—specifically flight path
optimization—represent a complementary and
underexplored avenue for mitigation.

The data selected—atmospheric profiles, flight trajectory parameters, population density distributions,
and modeled sonic boom exposure metrics—directly correspond to the variables that determine how
sonic booms form, propagate, and impact populations. By integrating these datasets within a
computational framework, the study can systematically compare baseline and optimized flight paths
under identical environmental conditions. This enables controlled experimentation that would be
impractical or impossible through empirical flight testing.
A quantitative optimization framework is particularly well-suited to this project because it allows the
research to:
●
●
●

Explicitly define performance constraints (e.g., speed, altitude, feasibility)
Minimize exposure metrics using objective cost functions
Evaluate outcomes using replicable numerical comparisons rather than subjective interpretation

Qualitative methods would be insufficient, as they cannot capture spatial distributions, magnitude
differences, or constraint-based trade-offs inherent to supersonic routing problems. A mixed-methods
approach is also unnecessary because the research goal is not to interpret human perception directly, but
rather to model and reduce exposure potential as a precursor to policy or design decisions.
Overall, this methodology aligns with the research question by enabling precise, repeatable evaluation of
whether optimized flight path planning can meaningfully reduce sonic boom exposure while preserving
realistic flight performance.

By framing sonic boom reduction as an optimization
problem rather than a purely structural one, this
research contributes to a more flexible and scalable
approach to supersonic flight feasibility.

Recording Findings:
Instead of logging simulation results for every
individual variable across every timestamp of the
simulation, which is far too data-hungry of a
solution for recording findings, initial conditions
and parameters for each simulation/scenario are
kept, allowing me to rerun the simulation and plot
or store specific variables I may later need.

Data Sources:
Atmospheric profiles (U.S.): NOAA HRRR
Baseline atmosphere: U.S. Standard Atmosphere

Data (Simulation):
Three classes of routers are evaluated under identical
vehicle and operational constraints: (1) a baseline
fastest-route trajectory optimized for time, (2) a
population-avoidant supersonic route that penalizes
overflight of dense regions, and (3) a cutoff-optimized
supersonic route that penalizes predicted ground-reaching
acoustic rays. Route performance is compared using a
primary metric of population-weighted ground exposure,
alongside efficiency measures such as travel time and
distance.
Across matched atmospheric-route scenarios, the analysis
focuses on paired, scenario-based comparisons to
quantify exposure reduction, efficiency trade-offs, and
feasibility. Results are interpreted through effect sizes,
dominance relationships, and robustness across varying
atmospheric conditions rather than single-scenario
outcomes.
